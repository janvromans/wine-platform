Why you're overpaying for wine — and how to stop

Here's a fact that surprises most wine buyers: the same bottle of Chablis Premier Cru can cost anywhere from €28 to €46 depending on where you buy it in the Netherlands alone. That's a 64% difference for identical wine. And yet most people buy from the first shop they find.

We analysed pricing data across 35 European wine providers — from large retailers like Gall & Gall and Millesima to specialist Dutch webshops like Grandcruwijnen and Colaris. The price gaps we found were consistent and significant across almost every category.

Where the price gaps are biggest

Fine Bordeaux and Burgundy show the largest variation — up to 30% between the cheapest and most expensive provider for the same vintage. Everyday wines (€10–25) show smaller gaps of around 10–15%, but those savings add up quickly if you drink regularly.

Champagne is another category where comparison shopping pays off. A bottle of Bollinger Spéciale Cuvée ranged from €39 to €52 across our database. That's €13 saved on a single bottle — enough for a very decent second bottle of something else entirely.

Why do prices vary so much?

  • Buying power: Large retailers negotiate better deals with producers directly.
  • Stock age: Some shops hold older stock purchased at lower prices.
  • Shipping models: Free shipping thresholds vary wildly — €50 at one shop, €150 at another.
  • Promotions: Flash sales and loyalty programmes mean prices change frequently.

How to find the best price every time

The answer is simple: compare before you buy. Search the wine name on Vinora and see prices from all our providers side by side — including shipping costs. What takes 20 minutes of manual browsing now takes 10 seconds.

Our data shows that users who compare prices save an average of €8–12 per bottle on wines over €20. Over a year of regular buying, that's real money.

The 5 French wine regions every enthusiast should know

France produces more fine wine than any other country — and with that comes complexity. Dozens of appellations, hundreds of producers, and rules that can feel impenetrable. But at its core, French wine divides into five regions that explain most of what you'll find on a wine list.

1. Bordeaux

The world's most famous wine region is built on two grapes: Cabernet Sauvignon on the left bank (Médoc, Pessac-Léognan) and Merlot on the right (Saint-Émilion, Pomerol). Bordeaux is the home of the great châteaux — Margaux, Latour, Pétrus — but also produces enormous quantities of accessible everyday wine. Look for Bordeaux Supérieur for reliable quality under €15.

2. Burgundy

Where Bordeaux is about blending, Burgundy is about purity: single grape, single vineyard. Pinot Noir for red, Chardonnay for white. The region's complexity comes from its classification system — village, premier cru, grand cru — which reflects the specific plot the grapes came from. A Gevrey-Chambertin Grand Cru and a Bourgogne Rouge are both Pinot Noir, but worlds apart.

3. The Rhône Valley

Divided into north and south, the Rhône produces some of France's most compelling reds. The north is Syrah country — Hermitage and Côte-Rôtie are benchmarks of the variety. The south blends Grenache with Syrah and Mourvèdre, producing the rich, spiced wines of Châteauneuf-du-Pape.

4. Alsace

Alsace sits on France's eastern border with Germany, and its wines reflect that dual identity — French precision with Germanic aromatics. Riesling, Gewurztraminer and Pinot Gris dominate. Alsatian wines are almost always single-varietal and labelled by grape, making them unusually easy to navigate.

5. The Loire Valley

The Loire stretches 1,000km across central France and covers an enormous range of styles. Sancerre and Pouilly-Fumé produce benchmark Sauvignon Blanc. Muscadet is the go-to shellfish wine. Chinon and Bourgueil make elegant Cabernet Franc reds. Vouvray crafts everything from bone-dry to lusciously sweet Chenin Blanc.

Natural wine explained: hype or genuine revolution?

Walk into any wine bar in Amsterdam, Brussels or Berlin and you'll find natural wines on the list. But ask five people to define "natural wine" and you'll get five different answers. Here's what's actually going on.

What is natural wine?

There is no legal definition of natural wine — and that's part of the problem. In practice, the term describes wines made with minimal intervention: organic or biodynamic grape growing, hand harvesting, native yeasts for fermentation, no additives (or minimal sulphites), and no fining or filtering. The result is wine that reflects the vintage and the producer's vineyard as honestly as possible.

What makes it different?

Conventional winemaking uses a toolkit of interventions to produce consistent, stable wine at scale: commercial yeasts for predictable fermentation, sulphur dioxide as a preservative, fining agents to clarify, and sometimes additives to adjust acidity, colour or body. Natural winemakers reject most or all of these tools, accepting more variability in the result.

The hype problem

The natural wine movement has attracted genuine talent — producers like Marcel Lapierre in Beaujolais and Elisabetta Foradori in Trentino make wines of real complexity. But the label has also been applied to wines that are simply faulty: cloudy, volatile, oxidised, or unpleasantly funky. Not all natural wine is good wine. The key question is always whether the wine is interesting and enjoyable — not whether it carries the right label.

Should you try it?

Yes — with an open mind. Start with producers from established natural wine regions: Beaujolais (Morgon, Fleurie), Loire Valley (Muscadet, Saumur), and orange wines from Friuli or Georgia. Avoid bottles with no provenance or information. And remember: the best natural wines are defined by what they express, not what they exclude.

Champagne vs Prosecco vs Cava: which bubbles are worth your money?

Sparkling wine is one of the most price-variable categories in wine. A glass of Champagne at a restaurant can cost €18. A bottle of Prosecco at the supermarket costs €7. And Cava sits somewhere in between. Are you getting what you pay for?

Champagne

Champagne comes exclusively from the Champagne region of northeastern France, made from Chardonnay, Pinot Noir and Pinot Meunier using the traditional method — secondary fermentation in the bottle. This process creates the fine, persistent bubbles and the characteristic brioche, yeast and citrus character. Non-vintage Champagne from reliable houses like Bollinger, Taittinger or Pol Roger starts around €35–45 and represents genuine quality. Grower Champagne from small producers often offers better value at similar prices.

Prosecco

Prosecco is made in northeast Italy from Glera grapes using the tank method (Charmat), which is faster and cheaper than the traditional method. The result is lighter, fruitier and more approachable — peach, pear and white flower aromas, soft bubbles. Prosecco DOC is the entry level; Prosecco Superiore DOCG (from Conegliano Valdobbiadene) is meaningfully better and still usually under €15. For parties and aperitivo, it's excellent value.

Cava

Cava is Spain's answer to Champagne — made by the traditional method, primarily in Catalonia, from local grapes (Macabeo, Parellada, Xarel-lo). The flavour profile is earthier and more savoury than Champagne, with good acidity. Quality Cava is one of wine's great bargains: Reserva and Gran Reserva Cavas aged three years or more can rival entry-level Champagne at a fraction of the price. Look for the Cava de Paraje Calificado designation for the top tier.

The verdict

For everyday celebrations: Prosecco DOC or a good Cava Reserva (€10–18). For a special occasion: grower Champagne or a Cava Gran Reserva (€20–35). For a genuine gift or statement bottle: non-vintage Champagne from a serious house (€35–55). Compare prices across all three categories on Vinora before you buy.
